    content:
      title: "Study Summary"
      text: |
        ## Rationale
        In patients with acute hypoxemic respiratory failure (AHRF), preserving spontaneous breathing during mechanical ventilation offers physiological benefits, but also carries risks. While spontaneous breathing improves gas exchange and limits diaphragm atrophy, strong inspiratory efforts may worsen lung and diaphragm injury. Balancing these factors requires refined and tailored strategies, such as the modulation of PEEP. However, the impact of PEEP on neural respiratory drive and inspiratory effort is very heterogenous, and these two entities have only been studied separately in limited subsets of patients and healthy subjects. Additionally, it remains unclear whether the major determinant of PEEP-induced changes in respiratory drive and effort is represented by variations in diaphragm geometry, lung compliance, or by the presence of expiratory muscles recruitment, which may counteract its effect.

        ## Objective 
        The primary objective of the REEF study is to evaluate the effect of PEEP on neural respiratory drive and inspiratory effort in patients with acute hypoxemic respiratory failure, assessing diaphragm neuromechanical efficiency at different PEEP levels during invasive assisted mechanical ventilation.

        The main secondary objective is to determine the relative role of the major physiological contributors to PEEP-mediated effects.

        ## Study design and population 
        The REEF study is a prospective, physiological study conducted on invasively mechanically ventilated adult patients admitted to the ICU.

        ## Intervention 
        For each patient, six different PEEP levels (15-12-10-8-5-2 cmH2O) will be tested during a decremental PEEP trial with 30 minutes per step. During each step, neural respiratory drive, inspiratory effort, expiratory muscle activity, lung inflation pattern through electrical impedance tomography, respiratory muscle geometry and function through ultrasound and surface EMG, gas exchange and hemodynamics data will be collected.

        ## Main study parameters/endpoints 
        The primary outcome will be the assessment of PEEP-mediated changes in diaphragm neuromechanical efficiency (NME), which combines the roles of neural respiratory drive and the generated inspiratory effort. Secondary outcomes will include the evaluation of PEEP-induced changes in lung volumes, partitioned respiratory mechanics, global and regional aeration patterns, diaphragm geometry, presence of active expiratory activity, gas exchange and hemodynamic parameters.

      title: "Physiology focus: PEEP during spontaneous breathing"
      text: |
        In patients with acute hypoxemic respiratory failure (AHRF), preserving spontaneous breathing during mechanical ventilation offers physiological benefits, including improved gas exchange and hemodynamics, and the limitation of diaphragm disuse atrophy(1–3). On the contrary, the presence of strong inspiratory efforts carries the risk of aggravating lung and possibly diaphragm injury(4–6). This delicate balance underscores the urgent need for refined strategies to modulate neural respiratory drive and inspiratory effort to promote a successful transition from controlled to assisted ventilation. 

        Positive end-expiratory pressure (PEEP) is a mechanical ventilator setting that controls the amount of pressure in the lungs at the end of expiration to keep alveoli open, improve oxygenation, and prevent atelectasis. Recent findings in animal models and a few clinical studies have shown that positive end-expiratory pressure (PEEP) may also modulate neural respiratory drive and inspiratory effort(7–11). PEEP modulation may represent a truly intriguing and potentially clinically useful strategy, as it is an intervention that does not require further invasive treatments or procedures, such as sedation titration or the use of neuromuscular blocking agents(12, 13).

      text: 
        "However, the results are not unambiguous: higher PEEP may either increase or decrease respiratory drive and inspiratory effort. Notably, previous research has mostly examined neural respiratory drive and inspiratory effort separately(7, 9), despite the fact that each provides distinct insights into the impact of such intervention. Measured together, a more comprehensive assessment of diaphragmatic neuromechanical efficiency can be obtained(8, 14).


        In previous works, including some from our own institutions, three major mechanisms were identified that determine the net effect of PEEP on neural respiratory drive and effort. First, alterations in diaphragm geometry, especially muscle shortening, affect neuromechanical efficiency of the diaphragm(8). Second, changes in lung compliance directly affect respiratory mechanics during spontaneous breathing(9). Third, the response to PEEP may be impacted by expiratory muscle recruitment, which counteracts its effects(10, 11). To date, these mechanisms have only been addressed separately in small subsets of healthy subjects and patients. Therefore, it remains unclear whether the major determinant of PEEP-induced changes in respiratory drive and effort is represented by changes in diaphragm geometry, lung compliance (possibly related to changes in alveolar collapse and overdistention(15)), or to the presence of expiratory muscles recruitment. 


        Understanding the impact of PEEP, and its mechanism, on modulation of neural respiratory drive and inspiratory effort is pivotal for a personalized ventilatory management and the improvement of patient care in the critical setting."
